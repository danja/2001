I think the "pinhead" version appears in Francis Bacon's Novum Organum,
though I don't have a reference; for him it is an instance of useless
investigation.

I am sure that the question as stated is a distortion of a position reported
by Aquinas. In Summa theol. I q.52 a.2 resp. he refutes those who
"crediderunt quod angelus non posset esse nisi in loco punctali - who
believe that an angel is not able to exist except in a place that is a
point," i.e. that is indivisible and has a site ("punctum est indivisibile
habens sitam"). This, of course, is the point (punctus) of Euclidean
geometry applied to the real, physical world (not just the ideal point of
geometrical discourse). No one was talking about a "pinhead," which only
grossly approximates the punctum Aquinas is talking about, because a pinhead
is divisible. Aquinas quickly disposes of the proposition that an angel
occupies an indivisible punctum: an angel, he says, is "indivisibile extra
genus quantitatis et situs existens," viz. it is indivisible without
reference to quantity and some real location. So for Aquinas, "how many
angels can dance on the head of a pin" is a meaningless question. If it was
ever debated subsequently, it was only raised as an exercise to see if the
respondent knew his Aquinas. Which is to say, it was not a serious
question, even if punctum is properly understood. The "pinhead" version is
still less serious, being most probably an ignorant (Renaissance humanist)
misunderstanding, if not a deliberate distortion.

Richard Kay
