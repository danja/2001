Semantic Web Related Books

It's rare that I get to visit a decent bookshop or library, and in any case a lot of the SW-related material available is a bit too obscure for most places. As a result I spend a lot of money on books (usually through Amazon) that are useless, unreadable or both. Here, in the hope that it might be of use to others in similar circumstances are a few books that I have found useful or interesting.

If you have any suggestions to add to the list in the subject areas below or anything else relevant (e.g. the list could do with some more on Topic Maps), please mail me - you might reduce the money I waste.

  • RDF
  • Knowledge Bases
  • Logic
  • Other AI (general, expert systems etc)
  • Other (data mining etc)

Weaving the Web

Tim Berners-Lee 2000 Harper Business

The definitive lay backgrounder, covering the history of the (semantic) web, and its 'Ultimate Destiny'. Provides interesting insights into the timbl namespace. Must-have book for lovers of techie autobiography - more readable than the source code to cwm.

RDF

Creating the Semantic Web with RDF

Johan Hjelm 2001 Wiley

Given the state of the art, this is a fine effort. Takes a practical approach, describing the use of available RDF tools. The style is quite informal, and entertainingly opinionated in places. Includes good introductory material on RDF/XML, and touches on a lot of related specs ( RSS, DC of course, but also things like KQML). Notably includes a chapter 'Device Descriptions and User Profiles', which I think we can take as being pretty authoratative, given the authors's role in the development of CC/PP. Contains some introductory material on reasoning, ontologies and agents. There isn't (yet) a better primer for the Semantic Web.

RDF Specification

Dan Brickley *, et al 2000 iUniverse

The RDF M&S and RDF Schema specs bound in paperback book form. Delightfully described by Mr. Brickley as a 'cheesy piece of shovelware'. I bought a copy because I'm seriously fed up of carting around and sorting through masses and masses of A4 copy, but unless you suffer from similar problems, just get a printout. On the other hand, if danbri brings out a book himself, buy it. (* not to be confused with the other visionary author, Dan Brinkley)

Professional XML Meta Data

Various 2001 Wrox Press

Usual Wrox style book with 11 authors (including myself) providing a variety of angles. Practically orientated, it contains quite a bit of code. Covers (from scratch) RDF, Topic Maps, MDL (Meaning Definition Language), extracting metadata from DBs, metadata architectures etc, etc. Good material, but reprehensibly thin on inferencing.

Professional XML

Various 2001 Wrox Press (2nd Ed.)

Contains a couple of relevant chapters : 'RDF' and 'RDF Code Samples and RDDL'.

Knowledge Bases

Knowledge Representation : Logical, Philosophical and Computational Foundations

John F. Sowa 2000 Brooks/Cole

A remarkable book. It goes from Aristotle right up to current ideas on logic and knowledge engineering, all covered in a highly authoritative but reasonably readable fashion. The style is academic, but it's well-illustrated and contains code snippets (Prolog, KIF etc) where appropriate. In parts it can be hard work, but this is largely due to the density of the information. Not surprisingly, Sowa's babies, Conceptual Graphs are well covered, but so is pretty much everything in the krep field (I'm sure I even saw a reference to RDF in there somewhere, though it's not in the index). For a better idea of what's in this book check out Sowa's site. Stongly recommended.

The Logic of Knowledge Bases

Hector J. Levesque & Gerhard Lakemeyer 2000 The MIT Press
Interesting & seems pretty original, but fairly heavy going as it takes a solidly mathematical approach. The authors build up from a fairly straightforward predicate logic, adding semantic extensions chapter by chapter to cover aspects of KBs. Covers things like ASK & TELL and a situation calculus.

Logic

The Language of First-Order Logic

Jon Barwise & John Etchemendy 1992 CLSI

Logic 101. Goes from simple intro to predicate logic up to Godel's Incompleteness (in brief). Includes (Windows) software for a blocks world on which wffs can be tested.

Computability and Logic

George S. Boolos & Richard C. Jeffrey 1980 Cambridge University Press

Fairly formal coverage of first-order logic and Turing Machines, recursion etc. (I've only got an old edition).

Communicating and Mobile Systems : The Pi Calculus

Robert Milner 1999 Cambridge University Press

Logic for Room 101 - not for the fainthearted! Largely formal mathematical description of a new calculus that can powerfully describe information systems from a communications point of view (hence this could be very useful for internet entities such as mobile agents). I would strongly recommend the tutorial first, unless you are a hardcore maths junky.

An Introduction to Non-Classical Logic

Graham Priest 2001 Cambridge University Press

Introduces various different logics (including fuzzy logic) in a clear & conscise fashion. Remarkably easy to follow, thanks mainly to the use of tableaux (which are introduced & described). Probably the only book I'd recommend with an introduction entitled 'Mathematical Prolegomenon'.

Other AI

It might be argued that some of the books below aren't particularly relevant, but my opinion is that logic/KB approaches alone won't be anywhere near adequate to provide the facilities envisaged in the Semantic Web concept. There are hundreds of other books that could go here - but I haven't read 'em.

Artificial Intelligence : A Modern Approach

Stuart J. Russell and Peter Norvig 1995 Prentice Hall (I believe a new edition is on the way)

Someone (danc?) commented that this was essential reading - I agree. It's an excellent, wide-ranging introduction. Strongly recommended.

Essentials of Artificial Intelligence

Matt Ginsberg 1993 Morgan Kaufmann

Slightly less comprehensive than Russell & Norvig, but pretty good all the same.

Expert Systems : Principles and Programming

Joseph Giarratano & Gary Riley 1989 (there are more recent editions) PWS

Introduces predicate logic, inference etc in a straightforward and clear fashion including quite a bit of coverage of probability and inexact reasoning. The second half of the book is a tutorial for the CLIPS expert system shell. (Note - I've only got an old copy of this, presumably a lot will have been updated).

Intelligent Database Systems

Bertino, Catania & Zarri 2001 Addison-Wesley

Though it does contain some theory, this is largely a review (with descriptions) of implementations. The first part of the book is an intro, the second part covers 'Efforts originated in a prevailing DB context' including material on relational & OO DBs, OMT, UML modelling, ODMG standard etc. The third part 'Efforts originated in a prevailing AI context' describes expert systems and moves on to deductive databases (e.g. DATALOG) and other such approaches, including an interesting discussion of Cyc.This section actually includes a little RDF - in discussion about an implementation of NKRL (narrative knowledge representation language). The final part, 'The Latest Developments' includes a smattering of these, e.g. temporal DBs, data mining, search engines.

Knowledge Acquisition from Databases

Xindong Wu 1995 Ablex Publishing Corporation

Appears like a bunch of academic papers, but contains good material on decision tree algorithms, a couple of knowledge base implementations and some tools for rule generation.

Constructing Intelligent Agents using Java

Joseph P. Bigus & Jennifer Bigus 2001 Wiley (2nd Ed.)

This looks really good - not had time to read it yet though...

Machine Learning

Tom M. Mitchell 1997 McGraw-Hill

Fairly formal treatment of learning theory, mainly concerned with neural nets, Bayesian techniques etc, but does cover rule learning and decision trees.

Object-Oriented Neural Networks in C++

Joey Rogers 1997 Academic Press

A good book for implementers comfortable with OO and with a little background knowledge of neural nets. Covers the most common nets with descriptions, formalizations & source code.

Introduction to Genetic Algorithms

Melanie Mitchell 1998 The MIT Press

A very good introduction, which actually goes quite deep and wide.

Professional Java Mobile Programming

Various 2001 Wrox Press

Only listed because it includes a chapter I wrote describing a simple expert system for the Palm, which you can find here.

Other

Data Mining

Ian H. Witten & Eibe Frank 2000 Morgan Kaufmann

A good practical introduction to machine learning in this context, plenty of this should be applicable to the web. The book is readable and has pretty good coverage. Java implementations of the tools are available from the web.
